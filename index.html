making better GIFs

Drop your images, hit Create, and you get a GIF. That's the whole thing.

But if you want it to feel right — to loop cleanly, move at the right pace, tell a tiny story — keep reading. Every control has a mouseover hint too.

Getting started

Drop images in any order

They sort by filename automatically. Drag thumbnails to reorder. Click × to remove.

Speed sets the rhythm

How long each frame shows. 200ms = quick, punchy. 600ms+ = dreamy, slow. Auto-suggested based on frame count.

Size = detail vs. file size

Bigger = sharper but heavier. 400–600px is great for sharing. 800+ for full quality. GIFs get big fast.

What these controls actually do

Dither

GIFs only get 256 colors. Dithering scatters dots to fake smooth gradients — like how newspapers print photos. On: almost always for photos. Off: pixel art, flat graphics.

Boomerang

Plays forward then backward, like a pendulum. That satisfying back-and-forth. Best with 3–8 frames of continuous motion.

Smooth loop

Cross-fades the last frame into the first so the loop point disappears. Try it: dancing, waves, anything that should feel endless.

Smart timing

Analyzes how much changes between frames and varies speed automatically. Big visual changes slow down. Similar frames zip by. Natural rhythm from your content.

Hold first / Hold last

Pauses 3× longer on that frame. Hold first for reveals ("wait for it..."). Hold last for punchlines. Both for dramatic pauses at each end.

Style presets

One-click color treatments applied at export. Your originals never change. Tonal presets (Film noir, Sepia) actually help GIF quality — fewer colors to compress. Rainbow/Sunset/Neon shift colors across frames.

Background elements

Procedural animations layered with your frames. Overlay blends on top of photos (rain, fireflies). Behind shows through transparent areas.

Crop

Center-crops all frames. Square (1:1) for Instagram. Widescreen (16:9) for Twitter. Portrait (9:16) for Stories.

Tell a tiny story

Title card = your opening line

Add a title card with text. It auto-detects faces for a portrait. Pick a style that sets the mood. The title gets its own frame — perfect for introducing what comes next.

Pace creates narrative

A GIF that starts slow (Hold first), speeds up, and pauses at the end (Hold last) has a beginning, middle, and end. That's a story. Even in 5 frames.

Frame order is editing

Drag frames to reorder. End on your strongest image. Or start with the conclusion and reverse — let the viewer discover how it happened.

Style as emotion

Golden hour = warmth, nostalgia. Midnight = drama. Faded = memory, distance. The preset sets the emotional register before the viewer processes the content.

Pro moves

The reveal

Title card → Hold first → 3–5 action frames → Hold last. Wait, watch, land. Works for before/after, recaps, product demos.

The infinite loop

Smooth loop ON + a sequence that starts and ends similarly. No jarring reset. Hypnotic. Great for ambient displays.

The boomerang bounce

3–5 frames of someone jumping, waving, any quick motion + Boomerang + fast speed. Classic satisfying loop. Turnaround eases automatically.

The mood piece

Slow speed (800ms+) + Film noir or Midnight + Customize: crank Vignette + Fireflies or Aurora. Simple photos become cinematic.

What to avoid

⚠ Too many frames at full size

20+ frames at 1200px = massive file. Social media will reject it. Reduce size to 400–600px, or cut frames.

⚠ Smooth loop + Boomerang

They fight each other. Boomerang already loops naturally. Pick one.

⚠ Backgrounds on busy photos

Overlay rain on a complex scene = visual noise. Backgrounds shine on simple subjects or slow sequences with breathing room.

⚠ Dither off on photos

Without dithering, photos get harsh color banding — flat blobs where gradients should be. Only off for pixel art or solid-color graphics.
